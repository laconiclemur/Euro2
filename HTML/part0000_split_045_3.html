Marzipan

 

Marzipan simply is a paste of almonds, sugar and rose water.  It is not so well known in the United States, but is a delicious treat in Germany year-round.  History is not clear from where the name originated.  It has been mentioned before Christ's birth, Nostradamus wrote about it in 1404, and since 1806 is has been commercially available.

 

In Europe today, it comes covered in chocolate bars, as candies in elegant boxes, as logs, dusted in cocoa as “potatoes,” and shaped as small painted fruit like bananas and strawberries.  Marzipan can also be infused with alcohol, surrounding centers in candies like Mozart Kugeln.  It comes shaped like hams, sausages and breads, and like pink piggies that bring good luck for the New Year.  A long time ago at Christmas, children used to be given a little toy grocery store, filled with a variety of food-shaped marzipan treats.  You can also buy unadorned blocks of Marzipan for blanketing tortes and forming roses or other decorations of your own.  Some examples of excellent commercially bought Marzipan are in the picture above.

 

Of course, there are many cookie recipes using it.  If you can’t find marzipan to buy, you can make your own.

 

·         2 cups finely ground almonds (put twice through an old-fashioned hand cranked nut mill)

·         2 1/2 cups powdered sugar

·         a few drops bitter almond extract or almond extract

·         2 Tbsp. rose water (available in Middle Eastern or Indian markets)

 

In a pan, mix all ingredients.  Over low heat, stir the paste constantly until it comes off the sides of the pan.  Wrap in a moist cloth and let it rest for 3 days.

 

Note - Don’t add too much bitter almond extract.  A little goes a long way.
