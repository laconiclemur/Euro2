LA CHARCUTERIE DE PROVENCE

The Cooked Meats of Provence

A selection of charcuterie may not seem traditional in a pantry, but it is lovely to have things like this handy and all keep very well, particularly the confit. When in France, to be honest, you are better off buying and keeping a selection from your local charcuterie or butcher. You can buy just a few slices at a time and get a few different types rather than making a lot of one. You’d be surprised by how much many French cooks, who are rumoured to be wonderful, rely on their boucher, traiteur or charcutier – not to mention ordering their desserts from the pâtisserie… There is no shame in this, it’s just another great example of common sense, leaving the cook to get on with what they do really well.

Failing such suppliers, these terrines are tried and tested recipes which will all last for at least five days. They all make a wonderful light meal with some pickled vegetables, toasted or grilled bread, thickly cut, and some salad. The duck confit can be carefully pulled out of its fat and slowly fried until the skin is thin and crisp and the inside melting. A warm potato salad with a dressing made using the duck fat, or potatoes fried in the duck fat, are the best accompaniment for confit. Make lots, I always do…
